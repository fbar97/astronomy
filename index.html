The Formation of our Solar System

About

Our solar system is more than 4.5 billion years old. It is made up of the 8 planets that orbit around the sun as well as well as their moons, asteroids, and comets. In order to understand the universe we live in, it is important to understand how the solar system was created.

Formation of the Sun

The sun, the center of the universe and the Earth’s main source of energy, has many stages in which it was formed. It is made of mostly Helium and Plasma, and is a large ball of plasma that is made up of charged ions. The enormous amount of energy that powers the star is produced through nuclear fusion, a process in which light nuclei such as Hydrogen combine to create bigger elements. But how did the sun form?

Stage 1:

Several billion years ago, a big Hydrogen cloud that contained remains of past dead stars began to collapse under gravity which may have been caused by a supernova explosion in the vicinity of the cloud. As the hydrogen cloud started collapsing, the cloud began to heat up because its potential energy converted into thermal energy.

Stage 2

Over time, a hot inner core began to form called the protostar as it became hotter and denser. The protostar was large enough that the collapse was able to continue and increase in temperature.

Stage 3

Eventually, the temperature of the protostar exceeded 10 million Kelvins which allowed the hydrogen nuclei to start fusing into Helium which is now the Sun’s source of energy output. The protostar became a star and its gravitational pressure reached hydrostatic equilibrium. The equilibrium was attained because the pressure increased along with the temperature and density. Hydrostatic equilibrium is thought to have occurred after 50 million years after the collapse. This stage is known as the sun’s Main Sequence.

Formation of the Planets

A planet is an object that orbits around the sun, is large enough to be round but small enough not to cause thermonuclear fusion, and does not have any surrounding planetesimals. Our solar system has eight planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. As an inhabitant of one of these planets, it is important to understand how they were formed!

Nebula

The first stage of planetary formation occurs after the formation of the star. The gravitational collapse of a star leads to a formation of a disk shaped cloud of dust and gas that surround the new star, called a nebula. The collapse causes the disk to rotate, and is held together by the gravity of the sun

The Disk

The gravitational collapse causes the radius of the nebula to decrease. The spinning cloud of dust follows the law of conservation of angular momentum. Since the radius decreased from the collapse, the disk's rotation gains speed, and the angular momentum remains the same. As the nebula collapses, the disk gains speed and flattens.

Disk Composition

The disk contains only 0.2% of the remaining mass. The most prevalent elements are :

  • 74% Hydrogen
  • 24% Helium
  • 2% other elements

The Disk (Continued)

The temperature and speed of disk particles depend on its radius from the center. Particles closest to the center have high temperature and rotational speed. As radius increases, the temperature and speed decrease. The spinning elements start to condense based on temperature. Rocks and metals condense at greater temperatures, closer to the center of the disk. Gases on the other hand require cooler temperatures, and therefore condense in the outer regions.

Accretion

As the disk continue to rotate and flatten, the tiny particles begin to collide and stick together, via electromagnetic force, forming small clumps. The clumps gather more surrounding particles as they continue to rotate, growing in size. As the clumps get big enough, it starts to have its own gravity that pulls and attracts smaller particles that surround it, forming small objects that are called “planetismals”. At first, hundreds of planetismals can be formed, all rotating around the disk as they use their gravity to attract small particles and get bigger. However, not all planetismals will end up as planets. Once these objects are big enough, a high speed collision between them would cause them to shatter. However, if they cross paths at slow and steady speed, their gravities would slowly merge them, forming larger objects. This process would continue for millions of years, where some planetismals get smashed, while others keep growing.

As mentioned before, the types of elements at different positions in the disk vary based on the distance from the center, creating planetismals with different properties. Rocky planetismals are formed close to the star, and icy planetismals are formed in outer regions where particles are mostly gas.

Stellar Winds

Rocky planetismals combine to form rocky planets such as earth and mars. Icy planetismals form Jovian or gas planets far away from the center. Finally, the sun send out a stream of energy called stellar winds. The stellar winds cleared the remaining dust and small particles, and blew off most of the close gases that were surrounding the rocky planets.

Formation of the Moon

The moon was formed 30-50 million years after the solar system was formed. It rotates around the Earth and causes its rise and fall of tides. There are several theories of how the moon was formed. We discuss a couple of them below:

Giant Impact Hypothesis:

The moon was formed when a mass the size of Mars collided with Earth. Chunks of this mass broke off and were drawn together by gravity into one mass circulating near Earth’s ecliptic plane. This mass become one of Earth’s moon.

Co-formation Theory

The co-formation theory suggests that Earth and its moon formed at the same time when two enormous masses collided. While this theory explains why they are near the same location it does not explain the differences in their density.
